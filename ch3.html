3.1 Elasticity

Elasticity governs the small-strain behaviour of soils but is assumed to remain present as long as effective stress continues to change, even within the large-strain range; its formulation is fundamental in developing constitutive models. The most physically consistent definition of elasticity would be linear elasticity where no energy dissipation occurs. However, such true elasticity is no longer valid for strains greater than 10−5 according to Yamazoe et al., 2014, which is the case in the present study. The discussion in this section therefore addresses generally non-linear “elastic” deformations beyond the small strain range.

3.2 Elasto-plasticity

The mechanical behaviour of soils, particularly soft soils, is inherently elasto-plastic, where plasticity signifies the component of deformation that is irrecoverable. To express this characteristic, the present chapter adopts the additive decomposition of the stretching tensor as follows:

$$\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}=\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^e+\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^p \tag{3.2.1}$$

Here, the superscripts “e” and “p” denote the elastic and plastic components, respectively. Similarly, the volumetric and deviatoric strain rates are decomposed as:

$$\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}=\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}^e+\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}^p \tag{3.2.2}$$ $$\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}=\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}^e+\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}^p \tag{3.2.3}$$

This section discusses several aspects of plasticity observed in experimental results and introduces their corresponding formulations.

3.3 Time-dependent deformation

Soils exhibit time-dependent deformation. It is primarily formulated as a soil–water coupled problem, in which the deformation of the soil skeleton and the pore water flow are solved simultaneously. The time-dependent deformation arising from the transient term in the pore water continuity equation is termed primary consolidation. In practice, it is associated with the measurable dissipation of excess pore-water pressure. Soft soils, however, exhibit additional time-dependencies that cannot be explained by primary consolidation alone (i.e., creep). This component is commonly referred to as secondary consolidation or, in the context of constitutive modelling, as viscosity. To express the time-dependent deformation, the additive decomposition of stretching is modified as:

$$\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}=\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^e+\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^{vp} \tag{3.3.1}$$ $$\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}=\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}^e+\dot{\varepsilon}_{nv}^{vp} \tag{3.3.2}$$ $$\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}=\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}^e+\dot{\varepsilon}_{nd}^{vp} \tag{3.3.3}$$

Here, the superscript “vp” denotes the viscoplastic component. This section discusses key features in primary and secondary consolidation of peats.
